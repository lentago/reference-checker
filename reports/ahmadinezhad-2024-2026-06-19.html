Section 6: Forensic Appendix

Methodology

This audit was conducted using the Forensic Reference-Integrity Auditor v5 against the 30-reference list of Ahmadinezhad et al. (2024), operated in Mode A (reference list only; no manuscript body text was provided for analysis). Each reference was subjected to live verification against Crossref (DOI resolution and metadata matching), PubMed and PMC (biomedical citation records and author verification), Retraction Watch (retraction notices and expressions of concern), publisher sites (direct archive verification of title, author list, volume, issue, and page data), and the NLM Catalog (journal founding dates and ISSN records). Eight heuristics were applied to every reference: H1 through H7, and H9. Heuristic 8 (sneaked reference) is unavailable in Mode A and was omitted. Risk tier assignments follow the Stage 4 rubric: High (H), Elevated (E), Moderate (M), and Defensible (D). No manuscript submission date was provided in the input; the H9 post-submission-date sub-check was therefore not applied. The H9 volume/issue plausibility sub-check was applied to all references with stated volume and issue numbers.

Scoring Explanation

Score = 100 − (H × 12) − (E × 5) − (M × 2) − (D × 3)
Score = 100 − (1 × 12) − (0 × 5) − (4 × 2) − (25 × 3)
Score = 100 − 12 − 0 − 8 − 75 = 5

The scoring formula applies a per-reference penalty to every tier, including Defensible references (D×3 per reference). This reflects the cost of residual editorial uncertainty — even independently verified citations carry a 3-point penalty because verification has inherent limits (database coverage gaps, grey-literature opacity, temporal coverage of founding-date data). A theoretical list of 30 perfectly verified references would score 100 − (30 × 3) = 10. The practical editorial maximum for a 30-reference list is therefore approximately 10.

For this list, the score of 5 should be read in context: 25 of 30 references are independently verified against Crossref, PubMed, and publisher records with no material discrepancies. The dominant score penalty is the D×3 term (75 points) from those 25 clean references. The single High-risk finding contributes 12 points; the four Moderate findings contribute 8 points. Editors should use the heatmap and suspicion index to identify the specific finding that requires action, rather than interpreting the raw score as an indicator of systemic integrity failure.

A score below 70 warrants escalation. A score below 50 indicates systemic integrity concerns. This list scores 5, which is below both thresholds — however, the forensic finding is isolated to one reference (Ref 6), and the score is mathematically bounded near zero for any 30-reference list regardless of quality.

Audit Limitations

  • Grey literature without URLs (Refs 4, 8, 16, 21, 22) cannot be fully independently verified beyond source-organization plausibility and title confirmation. Source organizations and general publication details were confirmed through web search.
  • Shadow-paper detection (H7) depends on database coverage. Very recent fabrications and articles from small or non-English-language journals may not be fully indexed in Crossref or PubMed. Coverage of historical Iranian journal archives in particular is incomplete, which affects the confidence interval on the Ref 6 finding.
  • Sneaked-reference detection (H8) is not available in Mode A. A full manuscript submission would be required to check for orphan references.
  • Temporal impossibility checks (H9) depend on journal founding-date data in Crossref and the NLM Catalog. Coverage may be incomplete for smaller, newer, or recently renamed journals. For Ref 6, the founding date and volume sequence of the Iranian Journal of Pediatrics were confirmed through multiple independent sources including PMC archive records.
  • Homoglyph detection (H2) in this audit was performed by visual and character-sequence inspection. Systematic computational homoglyph analysis was not applied; very subtle lookalike substitutions may not have been identified.
  • This audit is a forensic tool, not a definitive determination of research fraud. All findings, including the High-tier finding on Reference 6, should inform editorial judgment and trigger the appropriate COPE investigation procedure — they do not constitute proof of intent to deceive.

Escalation Levels

  1. Author query — Contact the corresponding author for documentation. For Reference 6, request the original source document (physical copy, library access record, or DOI resolution screenshot) and ask the author to confirm the correct article title, volume number, and year. Author query is always the first step for all H- and E-tier findings.
  2. Editorial investigation — Convene an independent editorial review. Do not rely solely on author-supplied documentation for confirmed H-tier findings. For Reference 6, if the author cannot resolve the temporal impossibility and the absent database record, initiate investigation per the applicable COPE flowchart.
  3. Publisher or institution notification — For confirmed fabrication, notify the publisher and/or the author's institution per COPE guidelines. For a published article, consider a correction, expression of concern, or retraction as appropriate to the severity of the confirmed finding.
